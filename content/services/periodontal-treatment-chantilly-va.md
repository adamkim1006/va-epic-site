## Why Periodontal Care Matters

Periodontal disease affects the bone and soft tissue that support your teeth. When infection is allowed to persist below the gumline, the result can be attachment loss, recession, bone loss, mobility, and eventually tooth loss.

The challenge is that periodontal disease is not always dramatic at first. Many patients present with bleeding gums, bad breath, or mild sensitivity long before they realize there is deeper structural damage.

- Gingivitis may still be reversible with timely care
- Periodontitis requires diagnosis and treatment, not just routine cleanings
- Recession and bone loss often need targeted procedures to improve stability
- Maintenance matters just as much as the initial treatment phase

---
## How Treatment Is Chosen

Dr. Park does not treat every periodontal condition the same way. The right plan depends on what is driving the problem and whether the goal is disease control, tissue coverage, regeneration, or surgical access.

### Common reasons treatment plans differ

- Depth and distribution of periodontal pockets
- Whether bone loss is localized or generalized
- Recession severity and root sensitivity
- Tooth prognosis and restorative plans
- Smoking, medical history, and healing factors

### Examples of periodontal treatment pathways

| Situation | Possible approach |
| --- | --- |
| Early to moderate inflammatory disease | Scaling and root planing with biofilm control and maintenance |
| Persistent deep pockets | Pocket reduction surgery or additional surgical access |
| Recession and root exposure | Gum grafting and soft-tissue management |
| Bone defects around teeth or implant planning sites | Regenerative procedures and bone grafting |

The goal is to stabilize the mouth in a way that patients can actually maintain over time.

---
## The Long-Term Goal

Periodontal treatment is not only about stopping infection today. It is about creating a healthier environment that supports teeth, future restorative work, and easier home care over the long term.

That may mean preserving natural teeth when possible, preparing sites more carefully before implants, or sequencing treatment so the final restorative result has a stronger foundation.
